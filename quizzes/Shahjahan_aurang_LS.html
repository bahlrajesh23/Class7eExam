Mughal Emperors: Shah Jahan & Aurangzeb

Shah Jahan: Early Life and Rise

  • Real name: Mirza Shahab-ud-Din Muhammad Khurram
  • During Jahangir’s reign:
    • Malik Amber, chief minister of Ahmadnagar, recovered territories conquered by Akbar in the Deccan.
    • Bijapur and Golconda were also captured.
  • Prince Khurram (later Shah Jahan) was sent to the Deccan and recaptured the forts of Ahmadnagar.
  • Honored with the title Shah Jahan (Lord of the World) after his victories.
  • Reign: 1628 CE – 1658 CE

Achievements and Contributions of Shah Jahan

  • Patron of Art & Culture:
    • Encouraged scholars, philosophers, and artists, leading to a cultural boom.
  • Suppression of Rajput Revolts:
    • Effectively dealt with Bundela Rajput rebellions.
  • Historical Records:
    • Important works: Padshahnama by Abdul Hamid Lahori and Shah Jahan Nama by Inayat Khan.
  • Strong Administration:
    • Maintained strict control over governance and military.
  • Trade & Infrastructure:
    • Developed road networks linking Agra, Lahore, Delhi, and Ahmednagar.

The Taj Mahal

  • Built in memory of his beloved wife, Mumtaz Mahal.
  • Considered among the New Seven Wonders of the World.
  • Construction took around 22 years.
  • Cost: 3 to 4 crore rupees at that time.
  • Made of pure white marble and lavishly decorated with semi-precious stones.
  • Features:
    • Octagonal monument with a splendid dome and four minarets.
    • Set in a beautiful garden.
  • Shah Jahan and Mumtaz Mahal are both buried here.

Shah Jahanabad and the Red Fort

  • Shifted capital from Agra to Delhi in 1639 CE.
  • Built a new capital called Shah Jahanabad (Old Delhi).
  • Constructed the massive Red Fort using red sandstone.

Notable Structures in Red Fort

Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audience)

  • Located in Red Fort, Delhi.
  • Used by the emperor to meet courtiers and official guests.
  • Features:
    • Ceiling made of silver.
    • Marble walls decorated with gold and semi-precious stones.

Diwan-i-Aam (Hall of Audience)

  • Also located in Red Fort, Delhi.
  • Used for public audiences and hearing grievances.
  • Features:
    • Shah Jahan’s famous peacock throne at the center.
    • Supported by 12 emerald pillars, each with two peacocks studded with gems.
    • Took 7 years to complete.
    • Taken to Persia by Nadir Shah in 1739 CE.

Later Years and Succession

  • In 1657 CE, Shah Jahan fell seriously ill.
  • Rumors of his death led to a war of succession among his four sons.
  • Aurangzeb, his third son:
    • Defeated his brothers.
    • Imprisoned Shah Jahan in Agra Fort.
    • Crowned emperor in 1658 CE.
  • Shah Jahan remained a prisoner for 8 years until his death in 1666 CE at age 74.

Aurangzeb (Alamgir I): The Last Great Mughal Emperor

  • Ascended the throne in 1658 CE after a bloody war of succession.
  • Empire stretched from Kashmir (north) to Karnataka (south), Hindu Kush (west) to Chittagong (east).
  • Reign: 1658–1707 CE (about 50 years)
    • First 24 years: Focused on north Indian affairs.
    • Last 26 years: Lived in the Deccan, fighting the Marathas.

Policies and Challenges under Aurangzeb

  • Religious Policies:
    • Reimposed the jizya tax on non-Muslims.
    • Followed a strict Islamic code.
  • Conflicts:
    • Fought against Rajputs, Jats, Bundelas, and Sikhs.
    • Sikh leader Guru Tegh Bahadur was executed.
  • Maratha Resistance:
    • Prolonged wars against the Marathas led by Shivaji.
  • Economic & Military Strain:
    • Continuous wars drained resources and caused instability.
  • End of an Era:
    • Death in 1707 CE marked the beginning of the Mughal Empire’s decline.

Decline of the Mughal Empire

  • Aurangzeb, over 80 years old, refused to negotiate with the Marathas.
  • Continued campaigns against Maratha strongholds until his death in 1707 CE at Ahmednagar.
  • At his death, Marathas had become a major political power, threatening Mughal supremacy.

Consequences of Long Wars

  • Mughal treasury was drained; Deccan’s economy ruined.
  • Administration weakened in the north, leading to corruption and lawlessness.
  • Nobles became rebellious.

Reasons for the Decline

  • Weak successors after Aurangzeb.
  • Vastness of the empire made it hard to control.
  • Frequent wars of succession.
  • Policies of Aurangzeb.
  • Powerful and often rebellious nobility.

End of the Mughal Empire

  • The Mughal Empire lasted for over 3 centuries.
  • Its collapse led to intense rivalry among various powers.
  • Ultimately, the British emerged victorious, ruling India for nearly 200 years.
